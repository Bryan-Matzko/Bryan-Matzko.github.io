Software Developer

Sept 2018 - Dec 2018

Miovision

Currently I work on the legacy developement team at Miovision, a company looking to aid in the developement of smart cities through the use of traffic data and traffic operations. My team's goal is to improve the tooling of older products supported by the company. The legacy team helps improves the performance of these older products and counters, we also look to make the customer experience more enjoyable. The majority of my work revolves around the company's QAP product, a tool that allows for counters to record traffic data from videos uploaded by external customers from the Company's traffic cameras. My main goal for QAP is to update the tool's workflow, improve performance, automate certain processes and support the data services team as issues and concerns rise. There are many exciting wins our team has obtained within the first two months of me working at this amazing company!

One big win was the creation of algorithms to provide counter's with intuitive default key bindings based on historically tracked data. The default keybindings reduce user setup time and are created for nearly all possible traffic intersection configurations. I also created processes that take into account a counter's historical count tasks and provides defaults that match their ecent personalized keybindings. One of my future goals is to find a way to provide counters with similar tasks, allowing for their historical defaults to have even greater benifits in the reduction of counter setup time.

Another large win is the improvements made to the company's auto acceptance algorithms. This algorithm takes into account the resultant data from both the counter's count data and the overlap data that is recorded by a more experienced counter. Historical data was analyzed to determine what is considered to be the thresholds for the company's data acceptance standards. The findings were used to create strict filtering on count results. The changes to the original algorithms reduced the number of data comparisons admins were forced to conduct manually all the while improving the processes that the data must be run through. Algorithms were also created to automatically trigger task redo creations on tasks whose data fell below a specified accuracy threshold. By autocreating redos, admins saved a lot more time allowing for the most stressful days to go by without the fear of missing turnaround times. Future improvements for these changes will require that we teach the computer how to accept more tasks, allowing us to handle the majority of recurring cases that happen in production.

Another one of my team's goals is to run weekly releases involving feature flags. These flags hide our incremental margin work to improve all aspects of QAP. As features are created and tested, the feature flags allow us to instantly provide our users with new features. These are two things that have never been done on QAP, especially during a busy count season I am working during.

FleetCarma

I spent two terms at FleetCarma working on the .NET team as a software developer. FleetCarma is a company with the goal of driving electric vehicle adoption around the world. Their main product was a device that plugs into the OBD port in any car. The device has the capability of recording vast data which is received and decoded using different services inside their technological pipeline. Spending 8 months at FleetCarma was amazing as I was able to touch a vast majority of the services and solutions the company has to offer. The majority of my work was backend development, however I did fiddle around with React every now and then.

Some of the tasks I worked on include the creation and improvements of the many microservices, APIs, Azure Functions, backend services and unit/integration tests. I had the opportunity of making large contributions to specific company programs/products such as the Fleet Management Portals, Smart Charge Rewards Program and the Smart Charge Manager Program. I was also able to work on vital components of the company's battery state of health calculator/algorithm and VIN decoder, both of which are key components of their products.

Some of my favorite work here focused on database improvements and migrations. I created a multitude of stored procedures, database migrations written in MS SQL and created/improved important tables. One of my larger database migrations involved refactoring related vehicle information tables to enable association of trim levels retrieved from VINs for all vehicles, a key factor in determining more granular vehicle data based on model specifications. I also created proof of concepts and created all the plans to remove the need for a seperate database used for analytics in the Fleet Management Portals. My plans allowed for OLTP and analytics workloads to use the same database. This allows for the real time operational analytics. All this was achieved through research into indices, columnstore indices and analysis of the current database schema. Performance after my proposed changes resulted in queries that ran in less than 1% of the original runtime whilst being more accurate in the obtained information.

Optical Heart Rate Sensor Investigation

Everyone who has met me knows that I am a fitness fanatic, I'm always looking for ways to improve in every aspect of my life. I am also one of the firm believers that a lot of information can be extracted from a wearable technological device that can provide insights to many aspects of health and performance. Because of this I was instantly entranced with learning how activity trackers and heart rate sensors work.

I began doing research into how optical heart rate sensors work. I bought an optical heart rate sensor and attached it to an arduino to determine what I could learn from the readings. I created algorithms to detect peaks in the data and determine heart rate variability based on RMSSD calculations. I loved learning about different theories and the backed research I read. My next goal with this side project is to prototype a similar optical heart rate sensor, attach it to a BLE nano and provide information via bluetooth to either a phone or computer. This work is all out of personal interest and my end goal is to determine how I can reduce the noise resulting in movement of a user wearing the device and reduce the device's size while performing addequately for my personal lifestyle.

Another thing I have been looking into with this project is the research that backs the proposed correlation between wearables and certain aspects of health. This includes how certain devices track sleep, heart rate, and calories burnt by device owners.
